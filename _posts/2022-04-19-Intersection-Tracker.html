Earlier this year, Mayor Eric Adams and DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez promised to upgrade 1,000 intersections across New York City for safety. These improvements could include installing raised crosswalks, leading pedestrian intervals, shortening crossings, or other interventions.

Raised Crosswalks

Raised crosswalks put crosswalks at the same height as sidewalks. Raised crosswalks increase visibility, improve accessibility, and serve as speed bumps that slow drivers.

Leading Pedestrian Interval

These show a walk sign for pedestrians before a green light to car traffic, improving visibility by allowing pedestrians to begin crossing the street before cars make turns across the crosswalk.

Bike Corral

Bike corrals serve "daylight" intersections, increasing intersection visibility for drivers, bike riders, and pedestrians. Their placement also helps prevent drivers from taking turns too quickly.

Left Turn Calming

DOT places rubber curbs, flexible plastic posts, or bollards along the path of the turn to encourage drivers to follow the intended path for the turn and slow drivers down.

Traffic Signal

Traffic signals give pedestrians a specific time to cross an intersection without waiting for a gap in traffic and clarify when drivers, cyclists, and pedestrians should proceed.

All-Way Stop

All-way stops allow pedestrians to cross an intersection without waiting for a gap in traffic and make crossing less chaotic for drivers and cyclists.

The city has not comprehensively published where intersection upgrades have taken place or what metrics are being used to determine which intersections get improvements, so Transportation Alternatives's Brooklyn Activist Committee is tracking the administration's commitment to meeting this goal.

About this data

This list of upgraded intersections is compiled from NYC Open Data's Raised Crosswalks, Leading Pedestrian Interval Signals (LPIs), Bicycle Parking (to get Bike Corral locations), Left Turn Calming, and Traffic Signal and All-Way Stop Study Requests datasets. There are some intersection changes that DOT could be implementing that we believe wouldn't show up in any of these data sets, including adding Barnes Dances (or any other non-LPI signal changes), pedestrian islands, and any bulb-outs that might not be classified as turn calming.
